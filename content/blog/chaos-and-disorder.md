If you’re working on a book—or any long form text—, you probably won’t start at page 1 line 1, adding line after line until you’re finished: The End. Instead, you’ll start at a random point, and your writing will be all over the place. It will be non-linear, and chaotic in nature.

This will be true on multiple levels:


	On the text level, where you‘ll jot down an ending before you even start the climax.
	On the structural level, where you‘ll write Chapter Six right after Chapter Three, or change it into Chapter Two, or remove it altogether, only to bring it back as Chapter Ten.
	And, most glaringly, at the level of aside material, such as research, ideation, annotation, and all the stuff you’ll write that will never end up in the final output.


Ulysses was built for exactly this. In fact, Ulysses got its name from one of the most complex and chaotic stories in history (which in turn was a modern take on one of the wildest and most chaotic travelogs ever recorded). So we feel you. And we take this whole thing serious.

The concept of sheets

Everything you’ll write, every text, every idea, note and comment, will be in what we call a “sheet”. On the surface, sheets are normal text documents, and for the most part, you can treat them as such: create a sheet, write your text, edit, finish, all good. But sheets go much deeper than that.

First, every sheet has additional layers, where you can attach notes, images, and keywords. Think of it as reference material you would clip to a piece of paper—post-its, pictures, flags, scribbles on the backside, stuff like that.

Second, sheets are meant to be shuffled, moved around, and set aside. Like classic paper within a stash of… classic paper. And while sheets can hold long, monolithic texts, they really are meant to be used as fragments; as small parts of bigger pieces, which then form the whole.

The concept of groups

All sheets live in what we call “groups”. Groups are similar to classic folders, but differ in two meaningful ways: 1) Groups have no fixed sorting order, so you can arrange your sheets whichever way you like. 2) Groups will always display all of their content, even the content of nested groups.

This allows for some very powerful organization of your content, as well as fine-tuned access to any part of your structure. For example, if you’re writing a book, you could


	create a group called Chapters

		– create multiple nested groups– one for each chapter– each holding a couple of sheets that – form the actual chapters
	select the “Chapters” group, and 
	have access to your whole book, or

		– select a nested group, and – only access one of your chapters, or

		    – just one of your scenes.


Of course, if you’re not writing much, or if your texts tend to be short in nature, all this will sound irrelevant. But the more you write, and the longer your texts and projects get, the more you’ll appreciate this approach. Because it allows you to quickly drill down, move up, zoom in and out, work on small parts, or oversee the whole thing.

You‘ll love this. Promised.
